## 4.1 What is the problem that you are trying to solve? Why is it important?
(1,500 character limit)

Burnout costs U.S. employers $125 to $190 billion a year. For a 1,000 person company, that translates to roughly $5 million lost to turnover, absenteeism, and productivity collapse, with per employee damage ranging from $4,000 for hourly workers to $21,000 for executives (CUNY SPH, 2025).

There is growing evidence that employers will pay for mental health solutions when outcomes are measurable. Spring Health published results in JAMA Network Open showing roughly 1.9x ROI. Headspace has shown measurable stress reductions in controlled trials. The category is validated.

But these solutions remain reactive and engagement dependent. They rely on users actively seeking help or consistently opening an app. Traditional EAPs reach 3 to 5% of employees. Even platforms with strong evidence lose engagement outside controlled settings. That engagement dependency is the structural weakness across the category.

Meanwhile the pressure keeps building: Gen Z now outnumbers Boomers in the workforce with 92% demanding employer mental wellbeing support (APA), healthcare costs are rising 10% in 2026, and the Surgeon General has issued a formal Workplace Mental Health Framework.

The corporate wellness market is $70B today, projected at $106B by 2029, yet the employers who need it most, those with 10 to 500 people, remain largely underserved. That is the gap we are building to fill, starting there and building fundamentals to serve organizations of any size.

## 4.2 What is your proposed solution?
(1,500 character limit)

YU is a behavioral intelligence platform that detects early changes in employee state through primarily passive signals and closes the loop by measuring whether its response actually worked.

The system combines two data layers. The passive layer draws from wearable devices (Oura, Garmin, Apple Watch, Whoop) capturing HRV, sleep, activity, and recovery continuously with zero effort. The active layer is a minimal 30 second daily check in covering mood, energy, and sleep, used for calibration rather than as the primary signal. Together they build an idiographic behavioral baseline for each person, designed by our CSO using digital phenotyping from her research at Boston Children's Hospital and Harvard Medical School.

When that baseline shifts across 3 or more consecutive days, signaling sustained deviation rather than a bad morning, the system flags it and delivers a CBT grounded micro intervention matched to the drift: a sleep adjustment, a breathing protocol, a recovery recommendation. Then it measures whether the baseline recovers and feeds the result back into the model.

That loop is what makes YU different. Passive signals, baseline, detect drift, trigger a low friction response, measure recovery. The product is about signal and adaptation, not content or engagement.

For employers, an anonymous dashboard shows team level trends with no individual data exposed. The architecture runs on local first AI designed so employee data stays within the deployment.

## 4.4 Who is the existing competition?
(1,500 character limit)

The wellness space has attracted over $3 billion in venture funding, yet we have not found a player serving small and mid size employers with a closed loop, detection first approach.

Lyra Health ($907M raised, $5.85B peak valuation) and Spring Health ($467M, $3.3B valuation) lead enterprise mental health but both require 200 or more employee minimums, long sales cycles, and dedicated benefits teams, making them structurally difficult for SMEs.

BetterUp ($570M raised) sells executive coaching at $75 to $250 per session while Modern Health ($170M raised) targets enterprise with a median ACV near $69K. Neither deploys across an entire workforce at SME price points.

Calm and Headspace pivoted from consumer to enterprise but remain primarily content libraries with limited behavioral detection or outcome measurement. Engagement tends to drop to single digits after the first month.

Wellhub (formerly Gympass, $847M raised, $4.2B valuation) aggregates gym access but offers limited mental health intelligence and no detection layer.

Traditional EAPs remain the default: 3 to 5% utilization, reactive by design, and largely invisible to the employer until the cost has already been paid.

Across this landscape the recurring gap is the same. These solutions are open loop: they offer a resource and rely on the user to engage, with limited ability to measure whether anything changed or to feed results back into the system. YU is being built to close that loop.

## 4.5 What is your competitive advantage over existing solutions?
(1,500 character limit)

Our advantage sits at three layers, and the combination is difficult to replicate by adding a feature to an existing product.

The architecture. To our knowledge YU is the first system in this category that closes the loop end to end: collect passive signals from wearables and phone data, build an individual baseline, detect meaningful drift, deliver a low friction response, and measure whether the system moves back toward baseline.

The clinical layer. Our detection model was designed by Aline Zimerman, PhD candidate in digital phenotyping at Boston Children's Hospital (Harvard affiliated) and licensed clinical psychologist. The baseline is idiographic, the intervention library is CBT grounded, and the line between meaningful behavioral change and noise is defined by clinical expertise. A 2026 Nature Medicine study found AI combined with a behavioral layer outperformed standalone models by 43%, which is the architecture YU is built on.

The economics. YU consolidates the EAP, the fitness stipend, and the engagement platform into a single behavioral intelligence layer at $5 to $15 PEPM, well below the $4,000 to $21,000 per employee cost of burnout.

The data flywheel. Each interaction refines the baseline. After 90 days YU understands behavioral patterns in ways hard for a new entrant to match. Longitudinal data creates switching cost and improving accuracy over time.

Local first AI keeps employee data within the deployment by design rather than as an afterthought.

## 4.6 What is your go-to-market strategy?
(1,500 character limit)

Phase 1, now through September 2026: Founder led sales in Boston targeting HR leaders at tech, biotech, and startup companies with 50 to 200 employees through LinkedIn, the MIT Sloan network, and in person demos. No paid marketing, no sales team. We do things that do not scale because the learning matters more than efficiency at this stage.

Each pilot is designed for signal validation rather than distribution: 1 to 2 companies, cohorts of 30 to 60 employees, running 60 to 90 days. The longer duration matters because we need enough time to establish a behavioral baseline and observe whether deviations are meaningful. Without that window we are not testing what we claim to be testing. Target: 3 to 5 pilots by end of summer.

The conversion path is straightforward. After the pilot the employer sees real drift and outcome data on the dashboard and the ask is: here is what we found, here is the cost to continue, here is the ROI against your turnover spend.

Phase 2, Q4 2026: Convert pilots to paid annual contracts and activate referral loops within Boston's HR community where leaders already know each other. Target: 10 to 15 paying customers.

Phase 3, 2027: Expand to a second city on validated unit economics and deepen the passive data layer with additional wearable integrations.

We learned from the Red Hat hackathon that larger enterprises need compliance infrastructure we are not ready to serve, so we sequence by readiness. The fundamentals we build now are designed to scale.

## 4.7 How do you plan to monetize this solution?
(1,500 character limit)

YU sells a Per Employee Per Month subscription to employers, positioned as a behavioral intelligence layer rather than a wellness benefit.

Starter at $3 to $5 PEPM includes passive signal collection, behavioral baseline, drift detection, micro interventions, and employer dashboard for startups and budget conscious SMEs.

Plus at $6 to $10 PEPM adds enhanced analytics, wellness recommendations, and priority support for companies consolidating existing wellness spend.

Enterprise at $10 to $15 PEPM adds HRIS integration, custom configurations, dedicated support, and outcomes reporting for larger organizations.

Pricing is anchored to recovered value. SHRM estimates replacement costs at 50 to 200% of salary, so at $8 PEPM for a 500 person company the annual contract is $48K and preventing just two turnover events at $50K each covers the entire investment.

As we validate the core loop, a second revenue layer can emerge from a wellness provider network where YU takes a 10 to 15% booking fee per session. But this layer comes after we prove the detection and response loop, not before.

The flywheel: more employers bring more data, better baselines produce better detection, better outcomes bring more employers. The subscription gets us to sustainability. The provider network is the path to scale once the core is proven.

Unit economics at pilot: 80% gross margin on software with LTV to CAC targeting 5:1 through founder led sales.
